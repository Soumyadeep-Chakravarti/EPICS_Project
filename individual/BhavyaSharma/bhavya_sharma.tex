\begin{center}
    \textbf{\Large Abstract}
\end{center}

\noindent Breast cancer continues to be a major health concern for women globally, and catching it early can make a huge difference in patient outcomes. For this project, our team set out to build a machine learning system that not only detects breast cancer accurately but also explains its reasoning -- something we felt was really important for medical applications.

\vspace{0.5cm}
\noindent We tested out several different ML models -- Logistic Regression, Decision Trees, SVMs, and Random Forest -- to see which ones worked best for classifying tumors as either benign or malignant. We also combined them using a soft voting ensemble to get more reliable predictions overall.

\vspace{0.5cm}
\noindent What I think sets our project apart is that we didn't just chase high accuracy numbers. We made sure the system could tell doctors \textit{why} it made a particular prediction, through confidence scores and feature importance analysis. In the end, Logistic Regression and SVM both hit 98.25\% accuracy, with ROC-AUC scores above 0.99 -- results we were pretty happy with.

\vspace{0.5cm}
\noindent \textbf{Keywords:} Breast Cancer Detection, Machine Learning, Ensemble Learning, Explainable AI, Classification, Healthcare Analytics

Eventually, I landed on the \textbf{Wisconsin Diagnostic Breast Cancer (WDBC) dataset} from UCI's ML Repository. It's been used in tons of research papers, so I figured it was a safe bet.

\subsection{Understanding Medical Attributes}

This was probably the most interesting part of my work. The WDBC dataset has 30 features extracted from cell images, and at first, I had no idea what half of them meant. I spent a good amount of time reading about fine needle aspirates (FNA) and how pathologists actually look at cell samples. Here's what I figured out:

\subsubsection{Geometric Features}
These are basically measurements of cell nucleus size:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Radius}: How far, on average, from the center to the edge
    \item \textbf{Perimeter}: The distance around the whole boundary
    \item \textbf{Area}: Pretty self-explanatory -- how much space the nucleus takes up
\end{itemize}

The cool thing I learned is that cancer cells tend to have bigger, more irregular nuclei. So these size measurements actually tell us something medically meaningful.

\subsubsection{Texture Features}
These describe how ``bumpy'' or irregular the cells look:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Smoothness}: How much the radius varies around the cell
    \item \textbf{Compactness}: Basically $\frac{perimeter^2}{area} - 1$ -- tells you if the shape is compact or spread out
    \item \textbf{Concavity}: How much the boundary curves inward
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Shape Features}
These capture the overall shape characteristics:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Concave Points}: How many ``dips'' are in the boundary
    \item \textbf{Symmetry}: Whether the nucleus looks the same on both sides
    \item \textbf{Fractal Dimension}: This one was tricky -- it measures how complex the boundary is
\end{itemize}

\section{PROJECT OVERVIEW}

\subsection{Problem Statement}
We wanted to build a system that could tell benign tumors from malignant ones, but also explain why it made each decision. Just getting a ``yes'' or ``no'' from a model isn't enough when you're dealing with something as serious as cancer -- doctors need to understand the reasoning.

\subsection{System Architecture}
We broke the project into four main pieces:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Data Layer}: Loading and cleaning the data (this is where my work fit in)
    \item \textbf{Model Layer}: Training different classification algorithms
    \item \textbf{Ensemble Layer}: Combining models using soft voting
    \item \textbf{Output Layer}: Generating metrics, plots, and explanations
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Results Achieved}
I'm pretty proud of what we accomplished:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Logistic Regression and SVM both hit 98.25\% accuracy
    \item ROC-AUC scores were above 0.99 for the best models
    \item We got 98.61\% recall, which means we barely missed any actual cancer cases
    \item Cross-validation showed the results weren't just lucky splits
\end{itemize}
